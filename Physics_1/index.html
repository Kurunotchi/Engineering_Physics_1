1. Core Concepts of Capacitors

A capacitor is a device that stores electrical energy in an electric field. It consists of two conductors separated by an insulating material called a dielectric. The ability of a capacitor to store charge is measured by its capacitance, denoted by \(C\).

The capacitance is defined as the ratio of the charge stored on one plate to the potential difference between the plates:

\[C = \frac{Q}{V}\]

where \(Q\) is the charge in coulombs and \(V\) is the voltage in volts. The unit of capacitance is the farad (F), where 1 F = 1 C/V.

Capacitors are used in electronic circuits for filtering, timing, energy storage, and coupling signals between stages of amplifiers.

2. Physics of Capacitor Operation

When a voltage is applied across a capacitor, charges accumulate on the plates. Positive charge builds up on one plate and negative charge on the other. The electric field between the plates is uniform if the plates are large and close together.

The capacitance of a parallel-plate capacitor is given by:

\[C = \frac{\kappa \epsilon_0 A}{d}\]

where \(\epsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\) F/m is the permittivity of free space, \(A\) is the area of each plate, \(d\) is the separation between plates, and \(\kappa\) is the dielectric constant.

The energy stored in a capacitor is:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} C V^2 = \frac{1}{2} \frac{Q^2}{C} = \frac{1}{2} Q V\]

This energy is stored in the electric field between the plates.

4. Energy Storage in Capacitors

Capacitors store energy in the electric field between their plates. The energy density in the field is uniform for parallel-plate capacitors.

The total energy stored is:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} C V^2\]

where \(C\) is the capacitance and \(V\) is the voltage across the capacitor.

Alternative forms:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} \frac{Q^2}{C} = \frac{1}{2} Q V\]

The energy density \(u\) (energy per unit volume) in the electric field is:

\[u = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon E^2\]

where \(\epsilon = \kappa \epsilon_0\) is the permittivity of the dielectric and \(E\) is the electric field strength.

For parallel-plate capacitors, \(E = V/d\), so:

\[u = \frac{1}{2} \kappa \epsilon_0 (V/d)^2\]

The total energy can also be expressed as:

\[U = u \times \text{volume} = \frac{1}{2} \kappa \epsilon_0 E^2 \times A d\]

Since \(C = \kappa \epsilon_0 A / d\), we get \(U = \frac{1}{2} C V^2\), as expected.

3. Dielectrics

Dielectrics are insulating materials placed between capacitor plates to increase capacitance. The dielectric constant \(\kappa\) (relative permittivity) is the factor by which capacitance increases.

\[C = \kappa C_0\]

where \(C_0\) is the capacitance without dielectric.

Dielectric Insertion with Battery Connected (V constant)

When a dielectric is inserted while connected to a battery:

  • Capacitance increases: \(C \to \kappa C_0\)
  • Charge increases: \(Q \to \kappa Q_0\)
  • Voltage remains constant
  • Electric field remains constant: \(E = V/d\)
  • Energy increases: \(U \to \kappa U_0\)

Dielectric Insertion with Battery Disconnected (Q constant)

When a dielectric is inserted after disconnecting the battery:

  • Capacitance increases: \(C \to \kappa C_0\)
  • Charge remains constant
  • Voltage decreases: \(V \to V_0/\kappa\)
  • Electric field decreases: \(E \to E_0/\kappa\)
  • Energy decreases: \(U \to U_0/\kappa\)

5. Series and Parallel Combinations

Capacitors can be connected in series or parallel to achieve desired equivalent capacitance values.

Series Combination

When capacitors are connected in series, they have the same charge Q, and the total voltage is the sum of individual voltages.

\[\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{C_i}\]

For two capacitors: \(\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2}\), so \(C_{eq} = \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2}\)

The equivalent capacitance is always less than the smallest individual capacitance.

Parallel Combination

When capacitors are connected in parallel, they have the same voltage V, and the total charge is the sum of individual charges.

\[C_{eq} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} C_i\]

The equivalent capacitance is the sum of all capacitances.

Voltage and Charge Distribution

  • Series: \(V_i = V_{total} \cdot \frac{C_{eq}}{C_i}\), \(Q_i = Q_{total}\)
  • Parallel: \(V_i = V_{total}\), \(Q_i = Q_{total} \cdot \frac{C_i}{C_{eq}}\)

Example

Two capacitors: \(C_1 = 2 \, \mu\text{F}\), \(C_2 = 3 \, \mu\text{F}\)

Series: \(C_{eq} = \frac{2 \times 3}{2 + 3} = 1.2 \, \mu\text{F}\)

Parallel: \(C_{eq} = 2 + 3 = 5 \, \mu\text{F}\)

6. RC Circuits

RC circuits consist of a resistor and capacitor in series. They exhibit exponential charging and discharging behavior.

Time Constant

The time constant \(\tau = R C\) determines the rate of charging/discharging.

Units: R in ohms, C in farads, \(\tau\) in seconds.

Charging

When a voltage \(V_0\) is applied to an initially uncharged capacitor:

\[V_c(t) = V_0 (1 - e^{-t/\tau})\]

The current decreases exponentially: \(I(t) = \frac{V_0}{R} e^{-t/\tau}\)

Discharging

When the capacitor discharges through the resistor:

\[V_c(t) = V_0 e^{-t/\tau}\]

The current: \(I(t) = -\frac{V_0}{R} e^{-t/\tau}\)

7. Engineering Considerations

Real-world capacitors have limitations and practical considerations that affect their performance and application.

Capacitor Types and Applications

  • Ceramic capacitors: Small, inexpensive, used for filtering and decoupling in digital circuits. Values from pF to µF.
  • Electrolytic capacitors: High capacitance (µF to F), polarized, used for power supply filtering and audio coupling.
  • Film capacitors: High voltage, low loss, used in power electronics and motor run applications.
  • Tantalum capacitors: High capacitance density, used in portable electronics.

Key Parameters

  • Voltage rating: Maximum voltage the capacitor can withstand without breakdown.
  • Temperature coefficient: How capacitance changes with temperature.
  • Equivalent series resistance (ESR): Internal resistance that causes power dissipation.
  • Dissipation factor: Measure of energy loss in AC applications.
  • Leakage current: Small current that flows through the dielectric even when fully charged.

Failure Modes

  • Dielectric breakdown: Insulation failure at high voltages.
  • Electrolytic dry-out: Loss of electrolyte in aluminum capacitors over time.
  • Thermal runaway: Excessive heating due to high ripple current.
  • Mechanical stress: Cracking of ceramic capacitors under thermal cycling.

Design Considerations

  • Derate voltage rating by 50-80% for reliability.
  • Consider temperature effects on capacitance and lifetime.
  • Minimize ESR for high-frequency applications.
  • Use appropriate capacitor types for the application (electrolytic for DC, ceramic for AC).
  • Consider size, cost, and availability constraints.

8. Problem-Solving Strategies

Effective problem-solving requires systematic approach and careful attention to units and formulas.

General Strategy

  1. Identify what is given and what is unknown. Draw circuit diagrams or capacitor geometries.
  2. Convert all units to consistent SI units (Farads, meters, seconds, Volts, Coulombs).
  3. For capacitor combinations: simplify networks step by step, replacing series/parallel groups with equivalent capacitors.
  4. Use fundamental relationships: \(C = Q/V\), \(U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2\), \(\tau = RC\).
  5. Check units and reasonableness of answers.

9. Worked Examples

Example 1: Parallel-Plate Capacitor

A parallel-plate capacitor has plates of area 0.04 m² separated by 0.002 m of dielectric with \(\kappa = 2.5\). Find the capacitance.

\[C = \frac{\kappa \epsilon_0 A}{d} = \frac{2.5 \times 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \times 0.04}{0.002} = 4.425 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{F} = 442.5 \, \text{pF}\]

Example 2: Energy Storage

A 10 µF capacitor is charged to 50 V. Find the stored energy.

\[U = \frac{1}{2} C V^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times 10^{-6} \times (50)^2 = 0.0125 \, \text{J}\]

Example 3: Series-Parallel Combination

Find \(C_{eq}\) for: \(C_1 = 4 \, \mu\text{F}\), \(C_2 = 6 \, \mu\text{F}\) in parallel, then in series with \(C_3 = 5 \, \mu\text{F}\).

\(C_{12} = C_1 + C_2 = 10 \, \mu\text{F}\)

\(\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_{12}} + \frac{1}{C_3} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{5} = 0.3\)

\(C_{eq} = \frac{1}{0.3} = 3.33 \, \mu\text{F}\)

Example 4: RC Circuit

An RC circuit has R = 2 kΩ, C = 47 µF. Find time constant and voltage after 0.1 s when charging from 0 V to 12 V.

\(\tau = RC = 2000 \times 47 \times 10^{-6} = 0.094 \, \text{s}\)

\(V(t) = V_0 (1 - e^{-t/\tau}) = 12(1 - e^{-0.1/0.094}) = 12(1 - e^{-1.064}) = 12(1 - 0.345) = 7.86 \, \text{V}\)

10. Practice Problems

Solve these problems to test your understanding. Answers are provided in the Summary section.

Problem 1

A parallel-plate capacitor has plates of area 0.02 m² separated by 0.001 m. The dielectric is mica (\(\kappa = 7\)). Find the capacitance.

Problem 2

A 5 µF capacitor is charged to 100 V. What is the stored energy? If the voltage is doubled, what happens to the energy?

Problem 3

Problem 4

An RC circuit has R = 1 kΩ and C = 20 µF. The capacitor is initially uncharged. A 10 V battery is connected at t = 0. Find the voltage across the capacitor after 0.1 s.

Problem 5

Problem 6

Two capacitors, 4 µF and 6 µF, are connected in parallel. This combination is then connected in series with an 8 µF capacitor. Find the equivalent capacitance.

11. Summary

This module covered the fundamental concepts of capacitors and capacitance, including their operation, energy storage, combinations, and RC circuits.

Key Formulas

  • Capacitance: \(C = \frac{Q}{V}\)
  • Parallel-plate: \(C = \frac{\kappa \epsilon_0 A}{d}\)
  • Energy: \(U = \frac{1}{2} C V^2 = \frac{1}{2} \frac{Q^2}{C} = \frac{1}{2} Q V\)
  • Series: \(\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \sum \frac{1}{C_i}\)
  • Parallel: \(C_{eq} = \sum C_i\)
  • Time constant: \(\tau = R C\)
  • Charging: \(V(t) = V_0 (1 - e^{-t/\tau})\)
  • Discharging: \(V(t) = V_0 e^{-t/\tau}\)

Important Concepts

  • Dielectrics increase capacitance by factor \(\kappa\)
  • Energy is stored in the electric field between plates
  • Series capacitors have same charge, voltage divides
  • Parallel capacitors have same voltage, charge adds
  • RC circuits exhibit exponential charging/discharging
  • Real capacitors have voltage ratings, ESR, and leakage

12. Answers to Practice Problems

Problem 1

\(C = \frac{7 \times 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \times 0.02}{0.001} = 1.241 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{F} = 1.241 \, \text{nF}\)

Problem 2

\(U = \frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 10^{-6} \times (100)^2 = 0.025 \, \text{J}\)

When voltage doubles to 200 V, energy becomes \(4 \times 0.025 = 0.1 \, \text{J}\) (increases by factor of 4).

Problem 3

\(\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{6} = 1\)

\(C_{eq} = 1 \, \mu\text{F}\)

Problem 4

\(\tau = 1000 \times 20 \times 10^{-6} = 0.02 \, \text{s}\)

\(V(0.1) = 10(1 - e^{-0.1/0.02}) = 10(1 - e^{-5}) = 10(1 - 0.0067) = 9.93 \, \text{V}\)

Problem 5

Charge remains constant: \(Q = 10 \times 10^{-6} \times 50 = 5 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{C}\)

Capacitance becomes \(4 \times 10 \times 10^{-6} = 40 \, \mu\text{F}\)

Voltage becomes \(V = Q/C = 5 \times 10^{-4} / 4 \times 10^{-5} = 12.5 \, \text{V}\)

Energy becomes \(\frac{1}{2} Q V = \frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 10^{-4} \times 12.5 = 3.125 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{J}\) (decreases)

Problem 6

\(C_{12} = 4 + 6 = 10 \, \mu\text{F}\)

\(\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{8} = 0.225\)

\(C_{eq} = \frac{1}{0.225} = 4.44 \, \mu\text{F}\)
